Step 1

Before you start: get set up.

Activate your accounts, gather your equipment, and check a few quick settings so you're ready to log in on day one.

Accounts you'll need

  • UNI (your TC login) — your unique network ID and single sign-on for nearly every TC system, including TC Gmail & Google Workspace (which also unlocks TC's institutional access to Gemini and NotebookLM), Canvas (TC's learning management system, where all your courses live), and Zoom (used for most live sessions — access it through Canvas when possible). Activate your UNI after paying your tuition deposit.
  • Student Portal — your gateway to registration, records, financials, and program information at student.tc.columbia.edu. New to it? Walk through the Navigating the New Student Portal guide.

Having trouble with your UNI?

If you're having issues activating your UNI, resetting your password, or anything else account-related, contact TCIT — call (212) 678-3300, Option 1 or email servicedesk@tc.columbia.edu.

Visit TCIT Website

Equipment checklist

  • A computer that meets Canvas's basic specs and runs a current browser (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Safari).
  • Zoom installed and tested at zoom.us/test.
  • Reliable internet — wired Ethernet if possible, strong Wi-Fi otherwise.
  • A webcam (built-in or external) and a headset with mic for clearer audio.
  • A quiet, well-lit space for live sessions.
Detailed browser & system specifications

Canvas supports current and first-previous versions of Chrome, Firefox, Edge, and Safari. For the smoothest experience, keep your browser updated and clear your cache regularly. Minimum recommended specs: 2 GB RAM, broadband connection of at least 512 Kbps, screen resolution of 1024×600 or higher. For exact requirements, see the Canvas computer specifications.

What if I'm studying from a different time zone?

TC follows Eastern Time. At the start of each semester, add all your course sessions and deadlines to your calendar in your local time. If a deadline is ambiguous, ask your instructor to confirm. For live sessions, align your schedule in advance — instructors cannot accommodate every time zone individually.

Accessibility & accommodations

If a disability or condition may affect how you participate online — vision, hearing, cognitive, chronic health — register with the Office of Access and Services for Individuals with Disabilities (OASID) early. They coordinate accommodations like extended time, captioning, and alternative formats.

Visit OASID Website

A quick online-readiness self-check

Five minutes of reflection can save you weeks of friction. You'll thrive online if you can answer yes to most of these:

  • I have a consistent, reliable internet connection and a quiet place to study.
  • I can set aside at least 8 hours per week per course, spread across multiple days.
  • I'm comfortable asking questions in writing (email, discussion boards, Canvas messages).
  • I can keep track of deadlines without daily in-person reminders.
  • I know who to contact when something isn't working — technically or academically.
Step 2

Navigating your courses.

Canvas is home base. Zoom is where you meet. Here's how to make both work for you — plus a few norms that help everyone learn together.

Canvas basics

You'll find your syllabus, readings, assignments, grades, and announcements in Canvas. Customize your Dashboard, use the Canvas Inbox for messages, and turn on notifications so you never miss an announcement.

Canvas QuickStart Guide

Zoom for live sessions

Most live (synchronous) sessions use Zoom. Access Zoom through Canvas for the smoothest experience, join a few minutes early with a working camera and mic, and use a headset when you can. If you miss a session, watch the recording and complete any follow-up reflection.

Step 3

Succeeding as an online learner.

Online learning rewards steady effort over last-minute pushes. Treat asynchronous work — discussion boards, recorded lectures, readings — like a standing appointment, not something you'll catch up on later. The students who thrive aren't the most talented; they're the most organized and the most connected.

Build a weekly rhythm

  • Block specific times in your calendar for each course — treat them like live classes, synchronous or not.
  • Plan on at least 8 hours per course per week, spread across multiple days.
  • Log in at least three times a week so you don't miss activity.
  • Protect a dedicated study space, even if it's one corner of a table.
  • Take short breaks every 45–60 minutes.
  • Draft assignments in your own tool, then paste into Canvas — and submit a day early when you can, since tech issues happen.

Stay connected & care for yourself

  • Introduce yourself in discussion forums early in the semester, and post early each week — you'll get more meaningful replies.
  • Reach out to classmates for study groups — Zoom, Google Meet, or a chat thread.
  • Email instructors or visit office hours freely. You're not bothering them.
  • Graduate study is demanding — reach out early if you're struggling.

Know the rules

Academic integrity

You're expected to uphold the standards in TC's Student Code of Conduct — which covers plagiarism, collaboration boundaries, assessment conduct, and the growing question of how generative AI may be used in your coursework. Every course and assignment can set its own expectations, so when anything is unclear — especially around AI use, citations, or group work — ask your instructor before you submit.

Read the Student Code of Conduct

Privacy & online conduct

Online classrooms are still classrooms. Treat Zoom sessions, Canvas discussions, and course materials the same way you would a face-to-face seminar — with respect for your classmates, your instructor, and the ideas shared in the room.

  • Don't record, screenshot, or share class sessions without your instructor's explicit permission — and, where classmates are visible or audible, without their consent too. This applies to live Zoom sessions, breakout rooms, and private chat messages.
  • Treat course materials as intellectual property. Slides, recordings, readings, and assignments are shared with you for learning — not for public posting, social media, or redistribution outside your course.
  • Protect your own data. Keep your UNI credentials private (never share them, even with classmates), use a strong password, and think carefully about what personal information you post in discussion forums or profiles.
  • Respect your classmates' privacy. What's shared in class — stories, opinions, personal experiences — should stay in class. Don't quote or repost classmates' contributions outside the course without their consent.

Artificial intelligence at TC.

TC provides institutional access to AI tools, thoughtful guidelines for their use, and a growing library of resources to help you build AI literacy. Here's how to use them well.

AI expectations vary from course to course. Your syllabus sets the ground rules — when anything's unclear, ask your instructor.

Responsible use: five practices

01

Ask first.

If your syllabus doesn't mention AI, assume it isn't allowed until you've confirmed with your instructor.

02

Verify everything.

AI tools can produce confident-sounding content that's wrong. Cross-check facts, quotes, and citations against original sources.

03

Protect private data.

Never paste personally identifiable information, student records, or confidential research into a consumer AI tool.

04

Disclose your use.

When AI is allowed, cite the tool, the model, and how you used it. When in doubt — disclose.

05

Enhance learning, don't shortcut it.

The goal of your program is your growth. AI is a tool — not a substitute for thinking.

These guidelines cover best practices, data privacy, and intellectual property. We strongly encourage all students to review them before using any AI tool.

Build your AI literacy

Drawing on student-focused frameworks from UNESCO, EDUCAUSE, and Penn State, aim to grow across four dimensions:

Human-centered mindset Understand when AI helps, when it hurts, and who's affected by the tools you use.
Ethics & responsible use Bias, fairness, privacy, environmental cost, and academic integrity.
Critical evaluation Verify facts, cross-check citations, and spot confident-sounding errors before you trust them.
Technical understanding How these tools are built, what they can and can't do, and how to prompt and iterate with them effectively.
